## 1. US OFAC Sanctions on Iran — Restricted vs. Permitted Sectors

### Current Sanctions Framework

The United States maintains a comprehensive sanctions programme against Iran
administered by the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC), Treasury Department.
The primary legal authorities are:

- **IEEPA** (International Emergency Economic Powers Act, 50 U.S.C. §§ 1701–1707)
- **Iran Sanctions Act 1996** (ISA), as amended
- **Comprehensive Iran Sanctions, Accountability, and Divestment Act 2010** (CISADA)
- **Iran Freedom and Counter-Proliferation Act 2012** (IFCPA)
- **Iranian Transactions and Sanctions Regulations** (ITSR), 31 C.F.R. Part 560
- **Executive Orders** 12957, 12959, 13059, 13599, and subsequent EOs

### General Prohibition (31 C.F.R. § 560.203–560.206)

US persons (individuals and entities, wherever located) and non-US entities
subject to US jurisdiction (e.g. those using US-origin software, US financial
institutions, US cloud infrastructure) are **broadly prohibited** from:

- Exporting, re-exporting, or providing services to Iran
- Facilitating transactions by foreign entities for Iran
- Processing US dollar transactions involving Iran
- Using US-hosted infrastructure to process Iranian transactions

⚠️ **AMEC-specific uncertainty:** Does AMEC Solutions qualify as a "US person"?
Does UTUBooking use any US-origin software, US cloud infrastructure (AWS is
US-headquartered), or US financial services? If yes, the general prohibition
applies regardless of where AMEC is incorporated.

### Sectors Specifically Restricted

- **Financial services**: US dollar clearing, correspondent banking, SWIFT access
- **Energy**: oil, gas, petrochemicals
- **Shipping and shipbuilding**
- **Arms and military**: absolute prohibition
- **Metals and mining**
- **Software for commercial surveillance** (separate EO restriction)

### Sectors With General Licences or Exemptions

Several OFAC General Licences (GLs) and statutory exemptions may be relevant
to a travel booking platform. See Section 3 for detail.

## 2. Travel and Tourism Sector — Is Hajj Booking Permitted?

### General Position

**There is no blanket exemption for the travel and tourism sector under ITSR.**
The general prohibition in 31 C.F.R. § 560.203 applies to "services" broadly,
which would include online booking platforms.

However, several specific carve-outs exist that may create a pathway.

### Hajj-Specific Religious Travel

The ITSR contains an exemption for **personal remittances** (31 C.F.R. § 560.550)
and for transactions **ordinarily incident to personal travel** to Iran. However:

⚠️ The travel exemption in § 560.556 covers travel **to** Iran by non-Iranians,
not the provision of commercial booking services by a third party to Iranian pilgrims
travelling **from** Iran.

The Hajj presents a specific question: Saudi Arabia and the Organisation of
Islamic Cooperation (OIC) have historically treated Hajj as a protected religious
obligation. However, OFAC has **not** issued a specific general licence for
commercial platforms facilitating Hajj bookings for Iranian pilgrims.

⚠️ **Key unresolved question for lawyers:** Does facilitating Iranian pilgrims'
Hajj flight and hotel bookings constitute "providing services to Iran" under
31 C.F.R. § 560.206? The answer depends on where the service is deemed to be
delivered, who the contractual counterparty is, and whether any US nexus exists.

### Analogous OFAC Guidance

OFAC issued guidance in 2019 clarifying that certain **personal travel** services
for Iranian-Americans are permissible. However, this covers US persons travelling
to/from Iran for personal reasons — not commercial platforms serving Iranian
nationals departing Iran for Hajj.

## 3. OFAC General Licence Categories That May Apply

### GL § 560.530 — Personal Communications Services

Permits the exportation of services incident to the exchange of **personal
communications** over the internet. This covers email, messaging, social media.
It does **not** cover commercial booking services.

### GL § 560.540 — Exportation of Certain Services and Software Incident to Internet-Based Communications

Extends 560.530 to software and services. Again focused on personal communications,
not e-commerce or booking platforms.

⚠️ Some legal scholars argue that an online booking interface used by an individual
to purchase personal travel could be characterised as a "personal communication"
tool. This is a minority view and **not settled law**. A lawyer must assess this.

### GL § 560.545 — News-Gathering Activities

Not applicable.

### GL § 560.556 — Travel to Iran

Permits US persons to engage in transactions related to **personal travel to Iran**.
The inverse — an Iranian national booking travel from Iran — is not clearly covered.

### Specific Licence Process

If no General Licence applies, AMEC could apply for a **Specific Licence** from
OFAC under 31 C.F.R. § 501.801. OFAC considers specific licences on a case-by-case
basis. For a Hajj booking platform:

- The humanitarian/religious nature of Hajj would be a strong argument in favour
- The commercial nature of the platform (revenue generation) may complicate the argument
- Processing time: typically **90–180 days**
- Success rate for Iran-related specific licences: historically very low (estimated
  <10% for commercial services) — though Hajj framing may improve prospects

⚠️ **Key uncertainty:** Whether to apply for a specific licence is itself a legal
strategy decision that requires a lawyer's guidance. Applying puts AMEC on OFAC's
radar. Not applying and proceeding without a licence is an OFAC violation.

## 4. EU Sanctions on Iran — Council Regulation (EU) 267/2012

### Framework

The EU maintains its own Iran sanctions regime under:
- **Council Regulation (EU) No 267/2012** (consolidated with amendments)
- **Council Decision 2010/413/CFSP**
- Implemented in member states; UK post-Brexit has its own regime mirroring EU

### Scope

EU sanctions focus on:
- Nuclear-related goods and technology
- Financial restrictions (asset freezes, transaction restrictions)
- Arms and related materiel
- Designated persons and entities

### Travel Sector Position

Unlike the US ITSR, EU Regulation 267/2012 does **not** contain a broad prohibition
on providing services to Iran. EU sanctions are **more targeted** — they focus on
designated entities/individuals and specific prohibited goods/sectors.

**Preliminary assessment:** A travel booking platform serving ordinary Iranian
civilians booking Hajj travel would **likely not** be directly prohibited under
EU sanctions, provided:
- No designated persons or entities are involved
- No prohibited technology is transferred
- Payments do not involve designated Iranian banks

⚠️ **However:** If AMEC processes payments involving Iranian banks (many of which
are designated under EU sanctions) or uses EU financial infrastructure, this creates
exposure even if the core booking activity is permissible.

⚠️ **Key uncertainty:** If AMEC Solutions is incorporated or has operations in an
EU member state, EU sanctions apply directly. If not, they may still apply to
EU-based investors, customers, or banking relationships.

### UK Post-Brexit

The UK's Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI) administers sanctions
mirroring EU Regulation 267/2012 under the Iran (Sanctions) Regulations 2023.
Similar analysis applies if AMEC has UK connections.

## 5. Comparable Cases — Have Other OTAs Operated in Iran Legally?

### Booking.com

In **2019**, Booking.com withdrew Iranian hotels from its platform following
increased US pressure and the re-imposition of sanctions after the Trump
administration's JCPOA withdrawal. Prior to that, Booking.com had listed Iranian
properties, relying on the view that its Dutch incorporation placed it outside
US jurisdiction. The withdrawal suggests that even non-US OTAs face practical
pressure from sanctions risk (particularly US-dollar processing and US investor
relations).

### Expedia / Hotels.com

Expedia ceased listing Iranian properties in 2012 following CISADA.
Has not returned as of this brief.

### TripAdvisor

Continued to display Iranian content (user reviews) on the basis that this
constitutes personal communication / information services under the GL framework.
Does **not** process bookings for Iran.

### HRS (Hotel Reservation Service — Germany)

German B2B platform; continued operating in Iran post-JCPOA until 2019
sanctions re-imposition. Used euro-denominated transactions through non-US banks.
Withdrew in 2019 citing banking access problems rather than legal prohibition.

### Sabre / Amadeus GDS

Both GDS platforms **explicitly prohibit** use of their systems for Iran-originating
bookings in their terms of service, citing OFAC compliance obligations. This is
**critical for UTUBooking** — UTUBooking relies on Amadeus GDS for flight search.
Using Amadeus to book flights for Iranian users may violate Amadeus's terms and
Amadeus's own OFAC obligations.

⚠️ **Critical finding:** Even if AMEC obtained OFAC clearance for its own platform,
the reliance on Amadeus GDS, AWS infrastructure (US-headquartered), and potentially
US payment processors would create third-party compliance barriers that may be
**impossible to resolve** without complete technical separation.

### Airbnb

Briefly attempted to list Iranian properties in 2016 under JCPOA. Withdrew after
OFAC guidance clarified that the platform model (US company, US dollar processing)
did not qualify under the personal travel exemption. Paid a **civil monetary penalty**
in an unrelated Iran matter in 2023 ($20M+).

## 6. White-Label Model via Iranian Partner — Does It Change the Analysis?

### The Theory

A white-label model where UTUBooking licenses its software to a locally incorporated
Iranian company (or a third-country company with Iranian ownership) to operate
independently, with no UTUBooking branding or direct service delivery, might
appear to distance AMEC from direct sanctions exposure.

### Why This Does Not Resolve the Problem

**OFAC's "facilitation" prohibition** (31 C.F.R. § 560.208) is broad: it prohibits
US persons from facilitating transactions by foreign entities that the US person
itself could not undertake. If UTUBooking cannot directly serve Iranian users, it
**also cannot** licence its platform, provide technical support, or receive royalties
from an Iranian-market operator — because doing so would constitute facilitation.

⚠️ Key risks of the white-label model:
1. **Licensing fees / royalties**: receiving payment from an Iran-connected entity
   is itself a prohibited transaction under ITSR
2. **Technical support and updates**: providing software updates, bug fixes, or
   technical services to an Iranian-serving operator may constitute "exporting
   services to Iran"
3. **Code repository access**: if the Iranian operator accesses a GitHub repo
   maintained by AMEC, this may constitute a prohibited export
4. **Shared infrastructure**: any shared cloud infrastructure (AWS, etc.) creates
   nexus — see Section 5 on Amadeus

**The white-label model does not eliminate sanctions risk. It may reframe it
but introduces additional complexity (facilitation, royalty payments, software export).
A lawyer must analyse the specific structure.**

## 7. Required Structure If Compliant: What Would This Look Like?

If lawyers determine that a compliant structure is achievable, the minimum
requirements would likely include:

### Corporate Structure
- Establish a **separate legal entity** with no US-person ownership, control,
  or management — possibly incorporated in a third country (e.g. Türkiye, Oman,
  or UAE with appropriate ring-fencing)
- Ensure no US-person board members, investors, or officers in the Iran entity
- Complete legal and financial separation from UTUBooking Ltd / AMEC Solutions

### Technical Separation (Non-Negotiable)
- **Separate cloud infrastructure**: cannot use AWS (US-headquartered) for Iran
  operations without specific OFAC licence; must use non-US cloud provider
  (e.g. Hetzner/EU, Alibaba Cloud/CN, local Iranian datacentre)
- **Separate codebase and repository**: no shared GitHub, no shared CI/CD pipelines
- **Separate monitoring**: no shared Grafana, PagerDuty, ELK — US-connected tools
  may constitute prohibited software export
- **Separate domain and branding**: cannot operate under UTUBooking brand without
  risking the entire UTUBooking platform's OFAC status
- **Separate payment processing**: must use non-US, non-EU payment rails;
  Iranian Shetab network or hawala — both have compliance challenges of their own
- **Separate GDS/flight data**: Amadeus and Sabre prohibit Iran use; would need
  Iran-specific flight data provider (e.g. direct airline API with Iran Air)

### Payment and Financial Isolation
- No US dollar transactions — Iranian Rial or crypto (crypto has its own OFAC issues)
- No US or EU correspondent bank in the payment chain
- Iranian banking system is largely cut off from SWIFT — payment mechanics are
  a separate legal and practical problem requiring banking counsel

### Compliance Programme
- Dedicated OFAC/sanctions compliance officer for the Iran entity
- Screening of all users against OFAC SDN (Specially Designated Nationals) list
- Regular audits and record-keeping for potential OFAC examination

### Ongoing Reporting
- Consider whether voluntary disclosure obligations apply if any inadvertent
  transactions occur
- Maintain legal hold on all Iran-related records for OFAC examination

⚠️ **Cost estimate for compliant structure:** Based on comparable OTA Iran
entry analyses, achieving full technical and legal separation would cost
**USD 2–5M in setup** (legal, infrastructure, compliance programme) with
**ongoing compliance costs of USD 500K–1M per year**. Given the 87,500 pilgrim
market size, the economics are marginal without also capturing domestic Iranian
travel demand — which carries higher sanctions risk.

## Appendix: Key Definitions

**US Person:** A US citizen, permanent resident, entity incorporated in the US,
or any entity owned/controlled by a US person — regardless of where they are located.

**OFAC SDN List:** Specially Designated Nationals list — individuals and entities
that US persons are prohibited from transacting with.

**Facilitation (31 C.F.R. § 560.208):** Prohibits US persons from approving,
financing, guaranteeing, or otherwise facilitating a transaction by a foreign
entity that the US person itself could not undertake.

**General Licence:** An OFAC authorisation that permits a class of transactions
without requiring individual approval.

**Specific Licence:** An individual authorisation from OFAC for a transaction
not covered by a General Licence. Requires application; no guaranteed approval.
